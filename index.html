MaJoy's Journal

Hi everyone, I wanna tell you my story, so back in my confusing days of grade 12, I had a hard time what course to take. Everybody told me what path should I take. My strand wasn't even in the ICT zone, and significant people around me expecting I would choose a course related to Filipino Language, Biology or Psychology because they always told me that I excel to that subjects. If being selfish is a choice, I should have choose Biology but being practical weighs more. So others suggested that IT is the best choice since it is indemand - you know what I mean.

So, fast forward to my first year in my course or should I say, the "What on Earth am I doing here?" program. I have no idea what is programming. It was like trying to learn a new language that my brain refused to understand. Honestly, when the first time I heard the word "coding" it sounded like something robots and aliens did in secret societies. My first year was a mix of frustration and questioning why I didn't choose a path where Ctrl + Z could undo my decisions. That first year was a hundreds of YouTube tutorials, blur of coding, debugging, and Googling error messages like a detective trying to crack a complicated case. I felt like I was drowning in a sea of binary code, desperately trying to stay afloat, plus no one dares to help me with my code so I had to do it on my own. The struggles continued into my second year. It was like I was stuck in a never-ending loop, constantly debugging my self-doubts and syntax errors. I even considered switching courses at one point. Then, I questioned my life choices, wondering if my decision to dive into the world of IT was a huge mistake. But I remember, the moment Python was introduced to us, idk, my heart moved a bit because it's too cute and easy to understand. However, there's also a language villain for me like C#. Late-night coding sessions turned into early-morning existential crises and the only thing that keeps me going was the thought that one day, somehow, it would all make sense. Just try to graduate and find work and earn money - my very Asian mindset.

And now, it's my third year. I know there's a problem when it's been three years in my course yet there is unclear vision in me, however, I do understand that I don't have a choice. It hurts when I heard people say "no choice", silly me coz' I'm using it right now as an excuse. As what they say "if the shoe fits, walk in it 'till the high heels break" and it's too late now to realize that it's painful... not being able to be who you want to be is painful. But I need to invalidate that feeling inorder for me to survive. Someone also told us that the life when we step in from the real world of IT is much way difficult than the struggles in class. I mean, since young, I had experienced and exposed to unbearable society, being humiliated, stares of judgment, people who tried to bring you down, people who limits you, people who tend to support but blocked all exits of hardships, people who will abuse your kindness that could turn you into evil and people who will manipulate you because they never ended who they want to be. I guess it's enivitable. Demo, I don't want to be like them. I want to be a person who, despite the imminent hardships, retains integrity and authenticity burns within me. I don't want to succumb to the negativity that surrounds me. The challenges may be unavoidable, but the way I respond to them is within my control. This third year is not just about navigating the complexities of algorithms and programming languages; it's about fortifying my character and sense of self within the dynamic IT landscape. As I stare at lines of code, I am reminded that, like them, I have the power to adapt, grow, and create something meaningful. The challenges may be fated, but they are also an integral part of the journey toward becoming a resilient, authentic individual in the vast and ever-changing world of IT.
